fire, contained

Mia Mylvaganam
mylvaganam@u.northwestern.edu
CS497 Digital Musical Instrument Design
@ Northwestern University
One day in the sixth grade I was home alone, making mozzarella sticks. I stepped away to attend to other business (the hit tv show Shake It Up) and when I returned, there was a 3-foot fire extending upwards from the pot, licking the spice cabinets above it.

Through this class I've developed and specified an interest in how instruments and installations can respond to the environment they're placed in, the environment that users bring to them, and other conceptualizations of internalizing external changing factors. To explore environmental interactions through an instrument of my own, after much deliberation, I chose fire.

This instrument responds to fire with fire-related sounds. First I employed randomness, a concept arguably distant from nature. The loud whoosh of a fire blooming plays at random time intervals between 0 to 100 seconds. As the instrument heats up in the presence of fire, the volume of a crackling campfire sample increases and slowly fills the space. But don't let it get too hot! When the sensor heats up significantly, the smoke alarm goes off, the speed of which can be controlled with the physical potentiometer, morphing the classic smoke alarm sound into a series of low-pitched background patterns (if that's the route you choose). It continues to beep until the instrument cools down enough.

here's a demo if you're interested

It's constructed very simply. The two physical controls are the outwardly-extending temperature sensor (for ease of manipulation and movement) and the breadboard-mounted potentiometer which controls the smoke alarm speed. This data is sent to a Max patch that produces three sounds: fire starting, crackling campfire, and smoke alarm.

In the process of making this instrument, I explored many different ways of interacting with it and ultimately decided on the safety and confinement of a candle. I wish there was a more immersive or engulfing performance that could be had with this instrument that would not be dangeorus, but I suppose compromise is necessary when working with fire.

While testing and performing, I've melted the plastic shell of the temperature sensor pretty irreparably. Luckily it still works, and nothing unsafe beyond that has transpired.

Feel free to add things about lessons learned and how you might go about making a version 2 of this.
